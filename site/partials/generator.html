Study info

This study is being conducted by Jamie Birch for the evaluation of his MSc Computer Science dissertation project. He is a student in the Engineering Department of the University of Bristol, under the supervision of Prof. Peter Flach.

The study consists of three parts:

  1. Taking a quiz to measure current level of comprehension of a topic [est. 7 minutes]
  2. Studying from a vocabulary list [as long or as short as you choose]
  3. Taking another quiz to measure any changes in comprehension of the topic [est. 7 minutes]

I shall be asking participants to fill in a small demographic survey (below) so that demographic effects may be measured. Note that nonetheless, most fields are optional.

Data stored is limited to: the user-provided demographic information; the website-provided access id; the website-generated vocabulary list; timestamps of events; and the performance during the website-generated quizzes as well as the quizzes themselves. Information on unstated things such as IP address will not be taken.

Data protection

All data begins and will remain anonymous (eg. phrasing in the dissertation will resemble the format of: "participant #3, who claimed a high level of environmental exposure") and confidential. Data will be stored on my password-protected home computer, which is accessible only by myself.

Participants may request a PDF copy of the final dissertation, which is scheduled to be submitted on 15th September and - to my understanding - will be stored in print in the University of Bristol library.

Participants have a right to have their data withdrawn from the study upon request until 20th August, whereupon the data shall be considered usable in the final dissertation write-up.

Kanji data

Two information fields are provided:

Meaning
Possible translations of the character into English terms.
Reading
All the various ways in which the character may be pronounced based on different contexts (excluding proper nouns, inclusion of which would make the database too large to submit!).

Study data

Four information fields are provided, generally available only for the Ministry of Education-recommended characters:

Frequency
A ranking of the frequency with which each of the characters are used (higher ranks are more frequent).
Stroke
The number of strokes used to draw the character.
JLPT Level
The level of the Japanese Language Proficiency Test under which the character falls.
Grade
The school grade in which native Japanese would learn the character.

Extra info

Four information fields are provided, generally available only for the Ministry of Education-recommended characters:

Henshall page
The page of Kenneth G. Henshall's 1988 character etymology dictionary (A Guide To Remembering Japanese Characters) under which the character may be found.
Henshall index
The index of the character under the same book.
Unicode
The Unicode point of the character.
JIS
The JIS point of the character.
